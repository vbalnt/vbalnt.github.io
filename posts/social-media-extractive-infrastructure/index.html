The Feed Without a Bottom

societytechnology

The early internet felt different not because people were better, but because the architecture was. Decentralized, slow, and ungoverned, it rewarded curiosity and was not skillfully engineered for attention. It had its own pathologies, including flamewars and gatekeeping, but at least the medium wasn't optimized against you.

Then came social media. These platforms may have started as tools for connection, but once engagement became the metric, they evolved into behavioral extraction and conditioning machines. Their goal became harvesting identity, attention, and emotion at planetary scale.

Of course social media isn't without value. It enables organizing, gives voice to underrepresented communities, and connects people across distances. None of that requires algorithmic manipulation.

Never ending scrolling

The format of the feed itself is problematic. The infinite scroll removes every natural stopping point, every moment where you might ask: "do I want to keep going?"

This isn't an accident. It's a design pattern borrowed from slot machines—what Natasha Dow Schull documents in Addiction by Design: keep the user in the loop, remove friction, eliminate the exit.

Then consider what fills that infinite scroll.

Feeds are not neutral or community voted (except in platforms like Reddit and HackerNews). They are optimized for mostly two metrics: engagement & revenue.

A study of Twitter's algorithm found that the algorithm selects for divisiveness, which in turn encourages engagement. A Yale study of 12.7 million tweets found that users who got more likes for outrage learned to express more outrage.

The platform doesn't reflect human nature, it reshapes it. Every like, share, and angry reaction becomes a training signal. Over years of daily use algorithms train people what to say, how to feel, what to care about. All to maximize engagement, which in turn maximizes revenue. People become a distorted engagement-optimized version of themselves.

Why it's hard to detox from the infinite scroll

Our brains evolved for scarcity: rare rewards, slow feedback, small tribes. Social media exploits this mismatch, flooding us with dense, precisely-targeted stimuli we were never designed to resist.

TikTok builds psychological profiles mirroring the Big Five personality model. Research shows the algorithm tracks millisecond-level pauses before you swipe, interpreting hesitation as latent interest. You don't need to like or comment. A brief pause reveals internal conflict, and conflict can be a gateway to engagement.

Meta-analyses link short form video to reduced attention span and impaired impulse control. The algorithm optimizes for engagement and cognition is collateral damage.

Consider the asymmetry: billions of users, each with one brain, against a few hundred engineers optimizing for engagement. The concentration of epistemic power is historically unprecedented.

The damage is worst for adolescents. The developing brain is uniquely vulnerable to social validation loops and uniquely unable to resist them. The U.S. Surgeon General has called for warning labels on social media. Jonathan Haidt's research suggests smartphone-based social media correlates with a sharp rise in adolescent depression and anxiety starting around 2012 (though the causal strength is debated).

The European Parliament identifies social media as a key risk to democracy and researchers describe an "epistemic crisis".

In a span of ~30 years, we got from a utopian version of the early "cyberspace", to Echo Chambers and Epistemic Bubbles.

GenAI: The even more dystopic next phase

It only gets worse.

Until now, feeds were constrained by supply: algorithms could only select from what humans created. Human content has two natural filters: first, people normally won't create things they don't want to be associated with, as reputation and shame act as a brake. Second, each piece of content was made for many people, not tailored for one.

GenAI removes both constraints. It can generate whatever maximizes individual engagement, with no accountability and no shame. And it can create content for an audience of one: YOU, perfectly tuned to your specific vulnerabilities.

Imagine a feed where every post is generated specifically for you. Not selected, but created on the fly. Images, videos, text, voices, all synthetic, all optimized for your engagement profile. Infinite content, perfectly tuned to your psychological vulnerabilities, adapting in real time as it learns what makes you pause, what makes you scroll, what keeps you watching.

The feed becomes a mirror that knows exactly what to show you to keep you scrolling, because it made it for you, milliseconds ago.

People worry (rightfully) about robots taking jobs: a dystopia for later. But the other dystopia has already arrived: algorithms that know you better than you know yourself, generating an endless stream of content engineered to capture attention you never consented to give.

The Regulatory Vacuum

Imagine if a pharmaceutical company caused this level of cognitive harm with a badly tested drug. It would face legal action and regulatory intervention.

Big Tech gets press releases and tax breaks. This is not a technological problem anymore, but a political choice to let corporations weaponize attention for profit.

The tools exist: algorithmic transparency requirements, fiduciary duties to users, strict age verification, bans on engagement-maximizing defaults for minors. The EU's Digital Services Act is a start. But meaningful regulation requires political will—and that will is easily lobbied away.

That architecture is gone, but the lesson remains: technology that respects human attention is possible. We built it once. The question is whether we have the will to demand it again.
