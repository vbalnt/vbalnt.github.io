How MRI Actually Works

physicsmedical

You lie in a tube. Magnets hum. Radio waves pulse. Twenty minutes later, a doctor looks at a picture of your brain. How does a magnet take a photograph of soft tissue?

The answer involves nuclear physics, Fourier transforms, and a beautiful piece of signal processing. Let's build an MRI from scratch.

The Nuclear Compass

Every hydrogen nucleus in your body is a tiny magnet—a proton with spin. Normally these spins point randomly, canceling out. But put them in a strong magnetic field (1.5-7 Tesla) and they align, like compass needles.

# Simulate proton spins aligning in magnetic field
np.random.seed(42)
n_spins = 200

# Before B0 field: random orientations
theta_random = np.random.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, n_spins)
phi_random = np.random.uniform(0, np.pi, n_spins)

# After B0 field: aligned along z (with some thermal distribution)
# At room temperature, slight excess pointing up
theta_aligned = np.random.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, n_spins)
phi_aligned = np.random.exponential(0.3, n_spins)  # Clustered near z-axis
phi_aligned = np.clip(phi_aligned, 0, np.pi)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5), subplot_kw={'projection': '3d'})

for ax, theta, phi, title in [(axes[0], theta_random, phi_random, 'No Magnetic Field\n(Random Orientations)'),
                               (axes[1], theta_aligned, phi_aligned, 'In 1.5T Field\n(Net Alignment)')]:
    # Convert to Cartesian
    x = np.sin(phi) * np.cos(theta)
    y = np.sin(phi) * np.sin(theta)
    z = np.cos(phi)

    # Draw spins as arrows
    ax.quiver(np.zeros(n_spins), np.zeros(n_spins), np.zeros(n_spins),
              x, y, z, length=0.4, normalize=True, alpha=0.6,
              color=plt.cm.coolwarm(z/2 + 0.5))

    # Draw sphere
    u = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 30)
    v = np.linspace(0, np.pi, 20)
    xs = 0.5 * np.outer(np.cos(u), np.sin(v))
    ys = 0.5 * np.outer(np.sin(u), np.sin(v))
    zs = 0.5 * np.outer(np.ones(np.size(u)), np.cos(v))
    ax.plot_surface(xs, ys, zs, alpha=0.1, color='gray')

    ax.set_xlabel('X')
    ax.set_ylabel('Y')
    ax.set_zlabel('Z (B₀ direction)')
    ax.set_title(title, fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
    ax.set_xlim(-1, 1)
    ax.set_ylim(-1, 1)
    ax.set_zlim(-1, 1)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('spin-alignment.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()

The net magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ points along the field. But aligned spins are invisible to us—we need to tip them.

The RF Pulse: Tipping the Spins

A radio-frequency pulse at the Larmor frequency ($\omega_0 = \gamma B_0 \approx 64$ MHz at 1.5T) tips the magnetization into the transverse plane. This is nuclear magnetic resonance.

# Simulate 90-degree RF pulse tipping magnetization
t = np.linspace(0, 10, 500)  # Time in arbitrary units

# Bloch equations solution for 90° pulse then free precession
# M starts along z, tips to xy plane, then precesses and decays

# During RF pulse (0 < t < 1): tip from z to y
# After pulse: precess in xy plane with T2 decay, recover toward z with T1

T1 = 8.0  # Longitudinal relaxation
T2 = 3.0  # Transverse relaxation
omega = 5.0  # Precession frequency (in our units)

Mx = np.zeros_like(t)
My = np.zeros_like(t)
Mz = np.zeros_like(t)

for i, ti in enumerate(t):
    if ti < 1:
        # During RF pulse: tip toward y
        angle = (np.pi/2) * ti  # Linear tip
        Mx[i] = 0
        My[i] = np.sin(angle)
        Mz[i] = np.cos(angle)
    else:
        # After pulse: precess and decay
        t_after = ti - 1
        Mxy = np.exp(-t_after / T2)  # Transverse decay
        Mx[i] = Mxy * np.sin(omega * t_after)
        My[i] = Mxy * np.cos(omega * t_after)
        Mz[i] = 1 - (1 - 0) * np.exp(-t_after / T1)  # Recovery toward 1

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))

# 3D trajectory
ax1 = fig.add_subplot(121, projection='3d')
ax1.plot(Mx, My, Mz, 'b-', linewidth=1.5, alpha=0.8)
ax1.scatter([0], [0], [1], color='green', s=100, label='Start (equilibrium)', zorder=5)
ax1.scatter([Mx[-1]], [My[-1]], [Mz[-1]], color='red', s=100, label='End', zorder=5)

# Draw unit sphere
u = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 30)
v = np.linspace(0, np.pi, 20)
xs = np.outer(np.cos(u), np.sin(v))
ys = np.outer(np.sin(u), np.sin(v))
zs = np.outer(np.ones(np.size(u)), np.cos(v))
ax1.plot_surface(xs, ys, zs, alpha=0.1, color='gray')

ax1.set_xlabel('Mx')
ax1.set_ylabel('My')
ax1.set_zlabel('Mz')
ax1.set_title('Magnetization Trajectory\n(90° pulse → precession → relaxation)', fontweight='bold')
ax1.legend(loc='upper left')

# Time series
ax2 = fig.add_subplot(122)
ax2.axvline(1, color='orange', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='RF pulse ends')
ax2.plot(t, Mx, 'r-', linewidth=2, label='Mx (signal)')
ax2.plot(t, My, 'g-', linewidth=2, label='My')
ax2.plot(t, Mz, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='Mz (recovery)')
ax2.fill_between(t, Mx, alpha=0.2, color='red')
ax2.set_xlabel('Time', fontsize=12)
ax2.set_ylabel('Magnetization', fontsize=12)
ax2.set_title('Free Induction Decay (FID)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax2.legend()
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax2.set_xlim(0, 10)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('rf-pulse-fid.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()
_web('rf-pulse-fid.png')

The oscillating transverse magnetization induces a voltage in a receiver coil—this is the MRI signal. But wait: every proton in your body is now singing at the same frequency. How do we know where each signal comes from?

Gradient Fields: Spatial Encoding

The key insight: if we add a magnetic field that varies in space, spins at different positions will precess at different frequencies.

$$B(x) = B_0 + G_x \cdot x$$

Position becomes frequency. We can literally tune into different locations.

# Visualize gradient field and frequency encoding
x = np.linspace(-10, 10, 500)  # Position in cm

B0 = 1.5  # Tesla
Gx = 0.01  # T/cm (10 mT/m is typical)

# Field strength varies with position
B_field = B0 + Gx * x

# Larmor frequency at each position (in MHz)
freq = gamma * B_field / 1e6

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(12, 10))

# Magnetic field vs position
ax1 = axes[0, 0]
ax1.plot(x, B_field, 'b-', linewidth=2)
ax1.fill_between(x, B0, B_field, alpha=0.3, color='blue')
ax1.axhline(B0, color='gray', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
ax1.set_xlabel('Position x (cm)', fontsize=11)
ax1.set_ylabel('Magnetic Field (T)', fontsize=11)
ax1.set_title('Gradient Field: B(x) = B₀ + Gx·x', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Frequency vs position
ax2 = axes[0, 1]
ax2.plot(x, freq, 'r-', linewidth=2)
ax2.fill_between(x, freq.min(), freq, alpha=0.3, color='red')
ax2.set_xlabel('Position x (cm)', fontsize=11)
ax2.set_ylabel('Larmor Frequency (MHz)', fontsize=11)
ax2.set_title('Frequency Encoding: Position → Frequency', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Simulate signal from two objects at different positions
ax3 = axes[1, 0]
t = np.linspace(0, 0.01, 1000)  # 10 ms of signal

# Two "voxels" at x = -5 cm and x = +3 cm
x1, x2 = -5, 3
f1 = gamma * (B0 + Gx * x1)
f2 = gamma * (B0 + Gx * x2)

# Their signals
s1 = 1.0 * np.cos(2*np.pi*f1*t) * np.exp(-t/0.05)
s2 = 0.7 * np.cos(2*np.pi*f2*t) * np.exp(-t/0.05)
total_signal = s1 + s2

ax3.plot(t*1000, total_signal, 'purple', linewidth=1, alpha=0.8)
ax3.set_xlabel('Time (ms)', fontsize=11)
ax3.set_ylabel('Signal', fontsize=11)
ax3.set_title('Combined Signal from Two Locations', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax3.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Fourier transform reveals positions
ax4 = axes[1, 1]
# Compute FFT
signal_padded = np.zeros(4096)
signal_padded[:len(total_signal)] = total_signal
spectrum = np.abs(fft2(signal_padded))[:len(signal_padded)//2]
freqs = np.fft.fftfreq(len(signal_padded), t[1]-t[0])[:len(signal_padded)//2]

# Convert frequency back to position
positions = (freqs/gamma - B0) / Gx

ax4.plot(positions, spectrum, 'green', linewidth=1.5)
ax4.axvline(x1, color='red', linestyle='--', alpha=0.7, label=f'Object 1 at x={x1} cm')
ax4.axvline(x2, color='blue', linestyle='--', alpha=0.7, label=f'Object 2 at x={x2} cm')
ax4.set_xlabel('Position (cm)', fontsize=11)
ax4.set_ylabel('Signal Intensity', fontsize=11)
ax4.set_title('FFT Reveals Spatial Positions!', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax4.set_xlim(-15, 15)
ax4.legend()
ax4.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('gradient-encoding.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()
_web('gradient-encoding.png')

This is the magic: the Fourier transform of the signal gives us the spatial distribution of protons. One gradient gives us a 1D projection. But how do we get a 2D image?

k-Space: The Fourier Domain of Your Body

Here's where it gets beautiful. Define:

$$k_x = \gamma \int_0^t G_x(\tau) d\tau$$

The signal $S(t)$ we measure is related to the image $\rho(x,y)$ by:

$$S(k_x, k_y) = \iint \rho(x,y) \, e^{-i 2\pi (k_x x + k_y y)} dx \, dy$$

The signal IS the Fourier transform of the image. We sample k-space, then inverse FFT to get the image.

# Create a phantom (simulated brain slice)
N = 256
phantom = np.zeros((N, N))

# Create brain-like phantom with different tissue intensities
y, x = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]

# Skull (outer ellipse)
skull = ((x/100)**2 + (y/80)**2) < 1
phantom[skull] = 0.3

# Brain tissue (inner ellipse)
brain = ((x/90)**2 + (y/70)**2) < 1
phantom[brain] = 0.7

# Ventricles (two small ellipses)
vent_left = (((x+25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
vent_right = (((x-25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
phantom[vent_left] = 1.0
phantom[vent_right] = 1.0

# Gray matter regions (higher intensity)
gm1 = (((x+50)/20)**2 + ((y+30)/20)**2) < 1
gm2 = (((x-50)/20)**2 + ((y+30)/20)**2) < 1
gm3 = (((x)/30)**2 + ((y+50)/15)**2) < 1
phantom[gm1] = 0.9
phantom[gm2] = 0.9
phantom[gm3] = 0.85

# Lesion (small bright spot)
lesion = (((x+30)/8)**2 + ((y-35)/8)**2) < 1
phantom[lesion] = 1.0

# Compute k-space (this is what MRI actually measures!)
kspace = fftshift(fft2(phantom))

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

# Original phantom
ax1 = axes[0]
im1 = ax1.imshow(phantom, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
ax1.set_title('Tissue Density ρ(x,y)\n(What we want to image)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax1.set_xlabel('x')
ax1.set_ylabel('y')
plt.colorbar(im1, ax=ax1, fraction=0.046)

# k-space magnitude (log scale for visibility)
ax2 = axes[1]
kspace_mag = np.log(np.abs(kspace) + 1)
im2 = ax2.imshow(kspace_mag, cmap='hot', origin='lower')
ax2.set_title('k-Space |S(kx,ky)|\n(What MRI measures)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax2.set_xlabel('kx')
ax2.set_ylabel('ky')
plt.colorbar(im2, ax=ax2, fraction=0.046)

# k-space phase
ax3 = axes[2]
kspace_phase = np.angle(kspace)
im3 = ax3.imshow(kspace_phase, cmap='twilight', origin='lower')
ax3.set_title('k-Space Phase ∠S(kx,ky)\n(Also measured)', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax3.set_xlabel('kx')
ax3.set_ylabel('ky')
plt.colorbar(im3, ax=ax3, fraction=0.046)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('kspace-phantom.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()

The center of k-space contains low frequencies (overall contrast). The edges contain high frequencies (fine detail). Every point in k-space contributes to the entire image.

Sampling k-Space: The Acquisition

Different MRI sequences sample k-space in different patterns. Let's simulate the classic spin-echo sequence: one line of k-space per RF excitation.

# Simulate k-space sampling trajectories

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 3, figsize=(15, 10))

# Create k-space grid
kmax = 128
kx = np.linspace(-kmax, kmax, 256)
ky = np.linspace(-kmax, kmax, 256)
KX, KY = np.meshgrid(kx, ky)

# 1. Cartesian (standard spin-echo)
ax = axes[0, 0]
for ky_line in np.linspace(-kmax, kmax, 32):
    ax.plot([-kmax, kmax], [ky_line, ky_line], 'b-', linewidth=0.8, alpha=0.7)
    ax.plot(-kmax, ky_line, 'go', markersize=3)  # Start of each line
ax.set_xlim(-kmax*1.1, kmax*1.1)
ax.set_ylim(-kmax*1.1, kmax*1.1)
ax.set_xlabel('kx')
ax.set_ylabel('ky')
ax.set_title('Cartesian Sampling\n(Spin Echo)', fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
ax.set_aspect('equal')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# 2. Radial
ax = axes[0, 1]
n_spokes = 32
for angle in np.linspace(0, np.pi, n_spokes):
    ax.plot([0, kmax*np.cos(angle)], [0, kmax*np.sin(angle)], 'r-', linewidth=0.8, alpha=0.7)
    ax.plot([0, -kmax*np.cos(angle)], [0, -kmax*np.sin(angle)], 'r-', linewidth=0.8, alpha=0.7)
ax.plot(0, 0, 'go', markersize=5)

# 3. Spiral
ax = axes[0, 2]
t_spiral = np.linspace(0, 8*np.pi, 1000)

    else:
        ax.plot([kmax, -kmax], [ky_line, ky_line], 'orange', linewidth=0.8)

                   arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', color='orange', lw=0.5))
    if i < len(ky_lines) - 1:
        ax.plot([kmax if i%2==0 else -kmax]*2, [ky_line, ky_lines[i+1]], 'orange', linewidth=0.5)
ax.set_xlim(-kmax*1.1, kmax*1.1)

# 5. Partial Fourier (uses symmetry)

From k-Space to Image: Reconstruction

Now the payoff. We take our sampled k-space data and inverse Fourier transform to get the image:

# Full reconstruction pipeline

# Use our phantom
N = 256
phantom = np.zeros((N, N))
y, x = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]

# Brain phantom
skull = ((x/100)**2 + (y/80)**2) < 1
phantom[skull] = 0.3
brain = ((x/90)**2 + (y/70)**2) < 1
phantom[brain] = 0.7
vent_left = (((x+25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
vent_right = (((x-25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
phantom[vent_left] = 1.0
phantom[vent_right] = 1.0
gm1 = (((x+50)/20)**2 + ((y+30)/20)**2) < 1
gm2 = (((x-50)/20)**2 + ((y+30)/20)**2) < 1
phantom[gm1] = 0.9
phantom[gm2] = 0.9
lesion = (((x+30)/8)**2 + ((y-35)/8)**2) < 1
phantom[lesion] = 1.0

# Simulate acquisition with noise
kspace_full = fftshift(fft2(phantom))
noise = (np.random.randn(N, N) + 1j * np.random.randn(N, N)) * np.max(np.abs(kspace_full)) * 0.02
kspace_noisy = kspace_full + noise

# Reconstruct
recon = np.abs(ifft2(ifftshift(kspace_noisy)))

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

ax1 = axes[0]
ax1.imshow(phantom, cmap='gray', origin='lower')

ax2.axis('off')

ax3.imshow(recon, cmap='gray', origin='lower')

What Each Part of k-Space Contributes

Different regions of k-space encode different spatial features:

# Show what different k-space regions contribute

N = 256
phantom = np.zeros((N, N))
y, x = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]
skull = ((x/100)**2 + (y/80)**2) < 1
phantom[skull] = 0.3
brain = ((x/90)**2 + (y/70)**2) < 1
phantom[brain] = 0.7
vent_left = (((x+25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
vent_right = (((x-25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
phantom[vent_left] = 1.0
phantom[vent_right] = 1.0
lesion = (((x+30)/8)**2 + ((y-35)/8)**2) < 1
phantom[lesion] = 1.0

kspace = fftshift(fft2(phantom))

# Create masks for different k-space regions
ky, kx = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]
kr = np.sqrt(kx**2 + ky**2)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 4, figsize=(16, 8))

# Different radii for low/high frequency cutoffs
radii = [10, 30, 60, 128]
labels = ['Very Low\n(r<10)', 'Low\n(r<30)', 'Medium\n(r<60)', 'Full\n(r<128)']

for i, (r, label) in enumerate(zip(radii, labels)):
    # Low-pass filter
    mask = kr < r
    kspace_filtered = kspace * mask
    recon = np.abs(ifft2(ifftshift(kspace_filtered)))

    axes[0, i].imshow(np.log(np.abs(kspace_filtered) + 1), cmap='hot', origin='lower')
    axes[0, i].set_title(f'k-Space {label}', fontsize=10, fontweight='bold')
    axes[0, i].axis('off')

    axes[1, i].imshow(recon, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
    axes[1, i].set_title(f'Image from {label}', fontsize=10, fontweight='bold')
    axes[1, i].axis('off')

# Add labels
fig.text(0.02, 0.75, 'k-Space', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold', rotation=90, va='center')
fig.text(0.02, 0.25, 'Image', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold', rotation=90, va='center')

Just the center 10 points give you a blurry blob—but the contrast is already there. Adding more of k-space sharpens the edges.

Undersampling and Artifacts

What happens if we don't sample enough of k-space? Aliasing—the signal wraps around:

# Demonstrate aliasing from undersampling

N = 256
phantom = np.zeros((N, N))
y, x = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]
skull = ((x/100)**2 + (y/80)**2) < 1
phantom[skull] = 0.3
brain = ((x/90)**2 + (y/70)**2) < 1
phantom[brain] = 0.7
vent_left = (((x+25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
vent_right = (((x-25)/15)**2 + ((y-10)/25)**2) < 1
phantom[vent_left] = 1.0
phantom[vent_right] = 1.0

kspace_full = fftshift(fft2(phantom))

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 4, figsize=(16, 8))

# Different undersampling factors
factors = [1, 2, 4, 8]

for i, factor in enumerate(factors):
    # Undersample in ky direction (take every nth line)
    kspace_under = np.zeros_like(kspace_full)
    kspace_under[::factor, :] = kspace_full[::factor, :]

    # Reconstruct (will have aliasing if undersampled)
    recon = np.abs(ifft2(ifftshift(kspace_under))) * factor  # Scale for display

    # Show k-space
    axes[0, i].imshow(np.log(np.abs(kspace_under) + 1), cmap='hot', origin='lower')
    axes[0, i].set_title(f'Every {factor}{"st" if factor==1 else ("nd" if factor==2 else "th")} line', fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
    axes[0, i].axis('off')

    # Show reconstruction
    axes[1, i].imshow(recon, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
    if factor == 1:
        axes[1, i].set_title('Full sampling\n(No artifact)', fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
    else:
        axes[1, i].set_title(f'{factor}x undersampling\n(Aliasing/wraparound)', fontsize=11, fontweight='bold')
    axes[1, i].axis('off')

plt.suptitle('Undersampling k-Space Causes Aliasing Artifacts', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', y=1.02)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('undersampling.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()
_web('undersampling.png')

This is why MRI takes time—you need to sample enough of k-space. Modern techniques like parallel imaging and compressed sensing allow undersampling by using clever math to fill in the gaps.

T1 and T2 Contrast

Different tissues have different relaxation times. By choosing when to sample, we can highlight different tissues:

# Simulate T1 and T2 contrast

# Tissue parameters (approximate values in ms)
tissues = {
    'CSF': {'T1': 4000, 'T2': 2000, 'PD': 1.0},
    'Gray Matter': {'T1': 1200, 'T2': 80, 'PD': 0.8},
    'White Matter': {'T1': 800, 'T2': 70, 'PD': 0.7},
    'Fat': {'T1': 250, 'T2': 60, 'PD': 1.0},
    'Muscle': {'T1': 900, 'T2': 50, 'PD': 0.9},
}

# Create a multi-tissue phantom
N = 256
phantom_T1 = np.zeros((N, N))
phantom_T2 = np.zeros((N, N))
phantom_PD = np.zeros((N, N))

y, x = np.ogrid[-N//2:N//2, -N//2:N//2]

# Background (air) = 0
# Skull/fat layer
skull = ((x/105)**2 + (y/85)**2) < 1
phantom_T1[skull] = tissues['Fat']['T1']
phantom_T2[skull] = tissues['Fat']['T2']
phantom_PD[skull] = tissues['Fat']['PD']

# White matter
wm = ((x/95)**2 + (y/75)**2) < 1
phantom_T1[wm] = tissues['White Matter']['T1']
phantom_T2[wm] = tissues['White Matter']['T2']
phantom_PD[wm] = tissues['White Matter']['PD']

# Gray matter (cortical ribbon)
gm = ((x/90)**2 + (y/70)**2) < 1
phantom_T1[gm] = tissues['Gray Matter']['T1']
phantom_T2[gm] = tissues['Gray Matter']['T2']
phantom_PD[gm] = tissues['Gray Matter']['PD']

# More white matter inside
wm_inner = ((x/70)**2 + (y/55)**2) < 1
phantom_T1[wm_inner] = tissues['White Matter']['T1']
phantom_T2[wm_inner] = tissues['White Matter']['T2']
phantom_PD[wm_inner] = tissues['White Matter']['PD']

# Ventricles (CSF)
vent_left = (((x+25)/15)**2 + ((y-5)/25)**2) < 1
vent_right = (((x-25)/15)**2 + ((y-5)/25)**2) < 1
phantom_T1[vent_left] = tissues['CSF']['T1']
phantom_T2[vent_left] = tissues['CSF']['T2']
phantom_PD[vent_left] = tissues['CSF']['PD']
phantom_T1[vent_right] = tissues['CSF']['T1']
phantom_T2[vent_right] = tissues['CSF']['T2']
phantom_PD[vent_right] = tissues['CSF']['PD']

# Signal equations
def T1_weighted_signal(PD, T1, TR=500, TE=15):
    """Short TR emphasizes T1 differences"""
    return PD * (1 - np.exp(-TR / T1)) * np.exp(-TE / 80)  # Assume fixed T2 for simplicity

def T2_weighted_signal(PD, T2, TR=4000, TE=100):
    """Long TE emphasizes T2 differences"""
    return PD * np.exp(-TE / T2) * (1 - np.exp(-TR / 1000))  # Assume fixed T1

def PD_weighted_signal(PD, T1, T2, TR=4000, TE=15):
    """Long TR, short TE - minimal T1/T2 weighting"""
    return PD * (1 - np.exp(-TR / T1)) * np.exp(-TE / T2)

# Generate weighted images
img_T1 = np.where(phantom_PD > 0,
                  T1_weighted_signal(phantom_PD, phantom_T1),
                  0)
img_T2 = np.where(phantom_PD > 0,
                  T2_weighted_signal(phantom_PD, phantom_T2),
                  0)
img_PD = np.where(phantom_PD > 0,
                  PD_weighted_signal(phantom_PD, phantom_T1, phantom_T2),
                  0)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))

ax1 = axes[0]
ax1.imshow(img_T1, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
ax1.set_title('T1-Weighted\n(TR=500ms, TE=15ms)\nCSF dark, fat bright', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax1.axis('off')

ax2 = axes[1]
ax2.imshow(img_T2, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
ax2.set_title('T2-Weighted\n(TR=4000ms, TE=100ms)\nCSF bright, pathology bright', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax2.axis('off')

ax3 = axes[2]
ax3.imshow(img_PD, cmap='gray', origin='lower')
ax3.set_title('Proton Density\n(TR=4000ms, TE=15ms)\nAll tissue visible', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax3.axis('off')

plt.suptitle('Same Anatomy, Different Contrast', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', y=1.02)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.savefig(_img('t1-t2-contrast.png'), dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight', facecolor='white')
plt.close()
_web('t1-t2-contrast.png')

T1-weighted images show anatomy (fat is bright, fluid is dark). T2-weighted images highlight pathology (fluid and inflammation are bright). Same physics, different timing.

The Complete Picture

Let's put it all together—from nuclear spins to final image:

Summary

MRI is Fourier transform imaging:

Step Physics Math
Align spins Protons in B₀ field Boltzmann distribution
Excite RF at Larmor frequency Bloch equations
Encode Gradient fields $\omega(x) = \gamma(B_0 + G \cdot x)$
Receive Oscillating magnetization $S(t) = \int \rho(x) e^{-i\omega(x)t} dx$
Reconstruct Inverse FFT $\rho(x) = \text{FFT}^{-1}[S(k)]$

The signal you measure IS the Fourier transform of the image. Sample enough of k-space, invert, and you've photographed the inside of a human body without cutting anything open.

That's not metaphor. That's actually how it works.
